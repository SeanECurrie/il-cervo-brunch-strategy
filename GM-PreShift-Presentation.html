Pre-Shift Presentation: Brunch Launch

GM speaking notes · To be delivered 1 week before first Sunday brunch · 12-15 minutes

How to use this: This is your script for the pre-shift meeting where you introduce the brunch program to the full FOH team. Read through it once before the meeting. You don't need to memorize it — use it as talking points and speak naturally. The handout (separate document) gets distributed at the end. The goal: everyone leaves knowing what makes this brunch different, what to say to guests, and feeling genuinely excited about it.

Part 1: Set the Tone ~2 min

Say something like:
"Alright everyone, thanks for being here. Next Sunday we're launching something new — Sunday brunch. But I want to be clear about something upfront: this is not just 'we added brunch.' We actually did real research on this — looked at every brunch restaurant in Milwaukee, studied what works, what doesn't, where the gaps are. And what we found is that we have a position that literally nobody else in the city has. I'm going to walk you through it because I want you to understand WHY this matters, not just what's on the menu."
Tone: Confident but not hype-y. You're sharing real information, not doing a pep rally. The facts speak for themselves.

Part 2: The Three Things Nobody Else Has ~3 min

Say:
"Here are three facts about our brunch. Not marketing spin — verified facts from the research:

Number one: we are the only Italian brunch in Milwaukee. We looked at every brunch spot in the city — 15 competitors. Not one of them does Italian brunch. Onesto, Sorella, Calderone Club — none of them serve brunch. We're first.

Number two: we have the highest brunch view in the city. We're on the 9th floor. The only other place that comes close is Cafe Benelux with a 3-story rooftop. Blu at the Pfister is on the 23rd floor but they only do cocktails, not brunch. Nobody serves brunch from above the 3rd floor except us.

Number three: our chef is a Food & Wine Best New Chef. That's one of the biggest awards in American cooking. Chef Mike was named to that list in 2010. He cooked at Blackbird in Chicago — a Michelin-starred restaurant — Jean-Georges in New York — three Michelin stars — and WD-50, one of the most famous restaurants of the last 20 years. He's the real deal."
Pause after each one. Let it land. If people react, let them. These facts should be surprising — most of the team probably doesn't know Mike's full background.
If someone asks "what's Food & Wine Best New Chef?": "It's basically the Oscars for chefs. Food & Wine Magazine picks the 10 best new chefs in America every year. It's the list that launched careers like David Chang and Bobby Flay. Mike was on it."

Part 3: The Menu — What Makes It Italian ~3 min

Say:
"You're going to get a handout with all the dish details, but let me hit the three items I want every table to hear about.

The 24 Carat Gold French Toast. Banana rum toffee, gold-dusted candied cashews, torched meringue. This is literally dusted in gold. It is the most photographable dish on this menu. Every table that orders it will have their phones out. That is free marketing for us. If someone doesn't know what to order, start here.

The Black Truffle Omelet. Whipped herbed ricotta, focaccia, beautiful salad. Twenty-two bucks. And here's the upsell — add caviar for twenty-five more. Nobody else in Milwaukee is doing caviar at brunch. When it's someone's birthday, anniversary, or they just want to treat themselves — 'have you thought about adding the caviar?' That's a forty-seven dollar omelet. That's a check average game-changer.

The Bacon Egg and Cheese Pizza. Ricotta, scrambled egg, crispy bacon — and you can add truffle. This is the group item. If there are three or more people at the table, this is what you suggest for sharing. It's fun, it's different, and it photographs great.

And notice something about this menu — focaccia is in everything. The burger bun, the meatball sub, the bruschetta, the omelet side. That's Italian. That's house-made. That's what separates us. Two items use dry aged beef — the burger and the meatball sub. Chef Mike dry ages the beef in-house. That's the kind of thing you mention. It shows this isn't a hotel restaurant doing brunch — it's a serious kitchen."

Part 4: The Stuff You Need to Know ~3 min

Say:
"OK, practical stuff. You're going to get asked these questions a hundred times on launch day, so let's get them locked in now.

Parking. This is the big one. Sunday street parking in Milwaukee is free. No meters, no time limits. Most people don't know this. When someone asks about parking, lead with: 'Sunday street parking is free on Juneau and the surrounding blocks.' The hotel garage also has a $10 validation. But free comes first.

'Where is the restaurant?' — 'Take the elevator to the 9th floor, turn right.' Simple. Everyone at the hotel front desk should know this too.

'Do I need a reservation?' — 'Reservations are recommended on OpenTable. We do accept walk-ins but Sundays can fill up.' And here's a fun competitive angle — most brunch spots in Milwaukee don't even take reservations. Blue's Egg has 60-minute waits. We don't. That's an advantage."
Action: Ask if anyone has questions about parking, directions, or reservations. Get these answered NOW so nobody's guessing on launch day.

Part 5: What I Need From You ~2 min

Say:
"Three things I need from every person on the floor on Sunday:

One: sell the Italian. Don't say 'it's brunch.' Say 'it's Italian brunch.' Mention the bomboloni. Mention the Spritz Tower. If a table orders only classic items and doesn't try anything Italian, we haven't done our job. The Italian dishes are what makes us different — push them with pride, not pressure.

Two: tell the story. When someone asks about the food or the restaurant, tell them about Chef Mike. Tell them this is the only Italian brunch in Milwaukee. Tell them about the view. You are the marketing on launch day. Every guest who leaves and tells a friend is worth more than any Instagram ad.

Three: help us get the word out. There's a review card going in every check presenter — a small card that says 'Loved brunch? Tell Google' with a QR code. You don't have to hard-sell it. Just make sure it's there. And at the host stand, there's an email signup. If you're walking a guest out and they loved it, mention it: 'Sign up for our list and we'll let you know about specials and events.' That's it."

Part 6: Fire Them Up ~1 min

Say:
"Look — a lot of restaurants launch brunch and it's an afterthought. Generic menu, no story, no energy. That's not what this is. We have a nationally recognized chef, food nobody else in the city is making, and the best view in Milwaukee. That's real. That's not marketing — that's what we actually have.

The only question is whether people know about it. And right now, they don't. That changes next Sunday. You are the people who make that happen.

I'm going to hand out a reference sheet that has all the dish descriptions, the talking points, the FAQ answers, and a checklist. Read it before Sunday. Keep it in your apron if you need to. Any questions?"
Now distribute the FOH Brunch Launch Handout. Give everyone a minute to flip through it. Answer any questions. Then close the meeting.

After the Meeting

Follow-up text to the team (Sunday morning before service):
"Good morning team — launch day. Three things to remember: (1) It's Italian brunch, not just brunch. (2) Free Sunday street parking. (3) Bomboloni and Spritz Towers to every table. Let's have a great first Sunday. 🇮🇹"
After launch Sunday (Monday): Quick group text with the numbers — how many covers, what sold best, any guest feedback worth sharing. Keep the team connected to the results. They'll care about Sunday #2 if they know Sunday #1 mattered.
