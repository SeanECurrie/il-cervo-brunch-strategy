The Big Question: How Italian Is This Menu?

Is this "Italian brunch" or "luxury brunch at an Italian restaurant"?

What's Italian About It

  • Focaccia — in the burger, sub, bruschetta, omelet side
  • Giardiniera — in the burger and meatball sub
  • Calabrian aioli — on the bruschetta
  • San Marzano sauce — on the veggie pizza
  • Provolone, asiago, parmesan, ricotta — Italian cheeses throughout
  • Meatball sub — the most overtly Italian item
  • Marinara — on the meatball sub
  • Two pizzas — pizza IS Italian

What's NOT Specifically Italian

  • Gold French toast — banana rum toffee is Caribbean/American
  • Steak and eggs — classic American diner elevated
  • Truffle omelet — French technique, Italian ingredient
  • Caesar salad — technically Mexican-American invention
  • Salmon gravlax — Scandinavian technique
  • BEC pizza — Italian base, American toppings
  • Dry aging — technique, not cuisine-specific
  • Caviar — Russian/French luxury association

The Italian elements are ingredients and technique (focaccia, giardiniera, Calabrian chili, Italian cheeses, pizza, marinara) — not traditional Italian dishes (no frittata, no shakshuka/eggs in purgatory, no bomboloni, no Italian pastries, no affogato).

This isn't a critique — it might actually be smarter. "Italian brunch" sets an expectation that this menu doesn't fully deliver on. "Chef-driven luxury brunch" sets an expectation that this menu CRUSHES. The Italian identity is the restaurant's DNA, not the brunch menu's headline.

Positioning options for Mike to consider:

  • Option A: "Italian brunch" — technically defensible (focaccia, pizza, meatball sub, Italian cheeses) but the menu's strongest items aren't Italian (gold French toast, truffle omelet + caviar, steak & eggs). Guests who come expecting Italian brunch might be confused.
  • Option B: "Chef-driven luxury brunch" — truffle, caviar, gold, dry aging, 9th floor, Food & Wine chef. The menu fully supports this. The Italian restaurant context adds depth but isn't the lead message.
  • Option C: Both — "Elevated Italian-inspired brunch from a Food & Wine Best New Chef." Acknowledges the Italian DNA without overpromising on traditional Italian brunch.

Our research still stands either way. No one in Milwaukee is doing ANY of this — not Italian brunch, not luxury brunch, not truffle/caviar brunch, not 9th floor brunch. The competitive gap is real regardless of which positioning Mike chooses.

Questions This Menu Raised

1. Is this the final menu or still evolving? The filename says "Drag Brunch 2.2.2026" — is this the drag brunch menu, the regular brunch menu, or both? If it's specifically for drag brunch, the regular Sunday menu might be different.
2. What about drinks? The menu is food only. Is there a separate brunch cocktail menu? Mimosas, Bloody Marys, spritzes? A Spritz program would strengthen the Italian identity significantly. The cocktail menu matters for check average and for marketing (cocktail photos perform as well as food photos on Instagram).
3. Any plans for a prix fixe option? Our research found a "$45 Boozy Brunch" referenced elsewhere (2 cocktails + entree + pastry + coffee). Is that still happening alongside a la carte? A prix fixe simplifies the decision for new guests and guarantees a minimum check.
4. Where are the Italian pastries? Il Cervo is an Italian restaurant. The dinner menu presumably has desserts. Is there any opportunity for a bomboloni, cannoli, or Italian pastry plate as a brunch add-on or starter? Even a small $8-10 pastry item would strengthen the Italian brunch claim significantly.
5. The truffle and caviar add-ons — how are they presented? The presentation matters as much as the product for marketing purposes. If the caviar comes in a tin with service, that's an Instagram moment. If the truffle is shaved tableside, that's theater. The visual experience is what generates the content that markets you.
